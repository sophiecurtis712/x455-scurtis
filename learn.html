LEARN

Birds are everywhere — in city parks, along the shoreline, even outside your window. They come in all shapes, sizes, and colors, each with its own story and set of behaviors. In a place like San Francisco, the variety is especially noticeable. Within just a few miles, you can move from ocean cliffs to quiet wooded areas to open grassy hills, and each environment brings a completely different group of birds. Some birds soar high above the city, riding currents of air, while others stay hidden in dense trees or forage quietly along the ground. Gulls, hawks, hummingbirds, and songbirds all share the same spaces, often going unnoticed unless you take a moment to look more closely. This section is designed to help you slow down and begin to notice those details — the way a bird moves, the sounds it makes, or how it interacts with its surroundings. Whether you’re completely new to birding or just curious about the wildlife around you, learning to observe birds can change the way you experience everyday places. What might seem ordinary at first quickly becomes full of life, movement, and small discoveries waiting to be found.

Birding is all about slowing down and noticing the world around you. You don’t need fancy gear. Just patience, curiosity, and a willingness to watch and listen.

Binoculars make it easier to see details from a distance, and apps like Merlin Bird ID can help you identify birds on the spot. Local bird pamphlets and field guides are also great for learning shapes, colors, and calls. Start in a nearby park, follow movement in the trees, and let your eyes and ears guide you — every sighting is a little discovery.

how to use binoculars

  1. Hold the binoculars up to your eyes and adjust them until you see a single clear image.
  2. Use the center focus wheel to sharpen the image.
  3. Find your bird with your eyes first, then bring the binoculars up without losing sight of it.
  4. Keep both eyes open to reduce strain and improve tracking.
  5. Move slowly and follow the bird’s movement to keep it in view.

questions to ask when identifying a bird


  • How big is it compared to a crow, pigeon, or sparrow?
  • What colors or markings stand out on the wings, tail, or head?
  • How does it move — hop, flutter, soar?
  • What sounds or calls does it make?
  • Where is it — wetlands, park, forest, or shoreline?
